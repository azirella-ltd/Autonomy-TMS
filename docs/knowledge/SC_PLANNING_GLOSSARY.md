# Supply Chain Planning Glossary for AI Assistants

**Purpose**: Enable an LLM to interpret natural language from supply chain professionals. Each entry provides the term, definition, and context indicators for disambiguation. Sourced from APICS/ASCM Dictionary (17th Ed), SCOR Digital Standard, and industry practitioner conventions.

---

## 1. Planning Hierarchy & Processes

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **S&OP** | Sales & Operations Planning | Monthly cross-functional process that balances demand, supply, and financial plans over an 18-36 month rolling horizon to produce a single agreed operating plan. | Strategic/tactical planning; when someone says "the S&OP number" they mean the consensus demand or supply figure approved in the last S&OP cycle. |
| **IBP** | Integrated Business Planning | Evolution of S&OP (Oliver Wight) that adds financial reconciliation, product portfolio management, and strategic alignment into one monthly decision cycle. | Used interchangeably with S&OP in modern contexts; IBP implies tighter financial integration. |
| **MPS** | Master Production Schedule | A time-phased statement of what specific finished products the company plans to produce, in what quantities and in which periods; drives MRP. | When planners say "the MPS" they mean the approved production plan at the finished-goods or family level. |
| **MRP** | Material Requirements Planning | Time-phased calculation that explodes the MPS through the BOM to determine component purchase and production requirements, netting against on-hand and scheduled receipts. | "Run MRP" = execute the netting/explosion logic. MRP outputs are planned orders (PO, MO, TO). |
| **MRP II** | Manufacturing Resource Planning | Extension of MRP that integrates capacity planning, shop floor control, and financial planning into a closed-loop system. | Historical term; largely subsumed by ERP. Used in conversation to distinguish from basic MRP. |
| **DRP** | Distribution Requirements Planning | Applies MRP-style time-phased netting logic across the distribution network to determine when and how much to ship from supply points to demand points. | "DRP explodes the network structure; MRP explodes the product structure." |
| **CRP** | Capacity Requirements Planning | Validates MRP planned orders against all work center capacities to identify overloads before committing to the plan. | Operational-level capacity check (weeks-months horizon). |
| **RCCP** | Rough-Cut Capacity Planning | Quick validation of the MPS against critical (bottleneck) resources only, using resource profiles or bill of resources. | Tactical capacity check — faster and coarser than CRP. "Does the MPS fit our key resources?" |
| **APS** | Advanced Planning and Scheduling | Software that uses algorithms and optimization to simultaneously plan demand, supply, inventory, and capacity with constraints. | Vendor category (Kinaxis, o9, SAP IBP, OMP). Implies constraint-based planning beyond basic MRP. |
| **ERP** | Enterprise Resource Planning | Integrated transactional system that records and manages business operations across finance, procurement, manufacturing, inventory, and order management. | ERP is the system of record; APS/SCP sits on top for planning. "It's in the ERP" = the master data or transaction exists. |
| **SCP** | Supply Chain Planning | The umbrella term for all planning activities from demand forecasting through supply planning, inventory optimization, and production scheduling. | General category, not a specific process. |
| **PP** | Production Planning | The process of determining production quantities and timing at an aggregate level to meet the demand plan within capacity constraints. | S&OP-level aggregation; feeds into MPS. |
| **PAC** | Production Activity Control | Shop floor execution that manages the release, sequencing, and tracking of manufacturing orders through work centers. | Also called "shop floor control." "PAC dispatches the MOs." |

## 2. Demand Planning & Forecasting

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Demand plan** | — | The consensus view of expected future demand (units by product by location by time period) that drives all downstream planning. | "The demand plan" = the approved forecast after S&OP demand review. |
| **Forecast** | — | A quantitative estimate of future demand based on historical patterns, market intelligence, and judgment; always wrong, the question is by how much. | Conversational: "the forecast" usually means the statistical baseline forecast. |
| **Statistical forecast** | — | Forecast generated by time-series algorithms (exponential smoothing, ARIMA, ML) from historical demand data without human adjustment. | "The stat forecast" = the algorithm's output before any manual overrides. |
| **Consensus forecast** | — | The single demand number agreed upon after incorporating statistical forecast, sales input, marketing plans, and customer intelligence. | This is what gets approved in S&OP Step 2 (Demand Review). |
| **Demand sensing** | — | Short-term (1-4 weeks) signal processing that uses recent POS data, orders, and shipments to adjust the forecast for the immediate horizon. | "Demand sensing says we're tracking 15% above plan this week." |
| **Demand shaping** | — | Actively influencing demand through pricing, promotions, or product availability to align demand with supply capabilities. | "We can shape demand by pulling the promotion forward." |
| **Independent demand** | — | Demand for a finished product driven by customers or the market, not calculated from another product's requirements. | Forecasted, not calculated. Contrasts with dependent demand. |
| **Dependent demand** | — | Demand for a component or material that is calculated from the BOM explosion of its parent product. | Not forecasted — derived from MRP. "Dependent demand for subassemblies." |
| **Forecast bias** | — | Systematic tendency to over-forecast (positive bias) or under-forecast (negative bias) over time. | "We have a persistent positive bias on that product family." |
| **FVA** | Forecast Value Added | Measures whether each touchpoint in the forecasting process (stat model, sales override, consensus) actually improves or degrades accuracy. | "The sales overrides are destroying FVA — they're making it worse." |
| **MAPE** | Mean Absolute Percentage Error | Average of absolute forecast errors as a percentage of actual demand; most common forecast accuracy metric. | "Our MAPE is 25% at the SKU-week level." Caution: undefined when actuals = 0. |
| **WMAPE** | Weighted MAPE | MAPE weighted by volume so that high-volume products count more; avoids distortion from low-volume SKUs. | Preferred over MAPE for aggregate reporting. |
| **MAD** | Mean Absolute Deviation | Average of absolute differences between forecast and actual demand in units; used for tracking signal calculation. | "MAD of 50 units" = on average we're off by 50 units per period. |
| **Tracking signal** | — | Cumulative forecast error divided by MAD; detects when a forecast is consistently biased (typically flagged at +/- 4). | "The tracking signal tripped — we need to re-examine the model." |
| **CRPS** | Continuous Ranked Probability Score | Gold-standard metric for evaluating probabilistic forecasts; measures the entire predicted distribution, not just a point estimate. | Used when evaluating P10/P50/P90 forecast ranges, not just single-point accuracy. |
| **Cannibalization** | — | When demand for a new product reduces demand for an existing product in the same portfolio. | "The new SKU is cannibalizing our core product by 20%." |
| **Intermittent demand** | — | Demand pattern with many zero periods interspersed with sporadic, unpredictable orders; common for spare parts and slow movers. | Standard time-series methods fail here; use Croston's or empirical PMF. |
| **Lumpy demand** | — | Intermittent demand where the non-zero quantities also vary significantly in size. | Worst case for forecasting; needs probabilistic approaches, not point estimates. |
| **Promotional lift** | — | The incremental demand above baseline caused by a marketing promotion or pricing action. | "The promo lift was 3x baseline." |
| **Baseline demand** | — | The underlying demand pattern after removing promotional and event-driven spikes. | "Separate baseline from promotional demand before running the stat model." |
| **Unconstrained demand** | — | Total demand the market would buy if supply were unlimited; ignores production/inventory constraints. | S&OP Step 2 starts with unconstrained demand before applying supply constraints. |
| **Constrained demand** | — | Demand limited by known supply, capacity, or inventory constraints; represents what can actually be fulfilled. | The output of S&OP supply review. |

## 3. Inventory Management

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **On-hand** | On-hand inventory | Physical inventory currently in the warehouse, available or allocated; the quantity you can touch. | "What's the on-hand?" = how much physical stock do we have right now. |
| **In-transit** | In-transit inventory | Inventory that has been shipped but not yet received at the destination; also called pipeline inventory. | "We have 500 units in-transit arriving Thursday." |
| **Allocated** | Allocated inventory | On-hand inventory that has been reserved for specific customer orders or production requirements but not yet physically picked. | Allocated stock is not available for new orders. |
| **Available** | Available inventory | On-hand minus allocated minus quality-hold; the quantity actually available for promising or consumption. | "Available" is what matters for ATP — not raw on-hand. |
| **Safety stock** | SS | Buffer inventory held to protect against variability in demand and/or supply lead time; set by policy (service level, days of cover, or economic optimization). | Conversationally "SS" or "safety stock." In DDMRP critique, SS creates false demand signals in MRP. |
| **Cycle stock** | — | The portion of inventory that is consumed and replenished in regular order cycles; driven by lot size. | Larger lot sizes = more cycle stock. Cycle stock = Q/2 on average. |
| **Anticipation stock** | — | Inventory built ahead of expected demand peaks (seasonal build, promotions, plant shutdowns). | "We need to pre-build 2 weeks of anticipation stock before the holiday." |
| **Decoupling stock** | — | Inventory positioned between process stages to allow each stage to operate independently. | DDMRP: "strategic decoupling points" in the network. |
| **Pipeline stock** | — | Synonym for in-transit inventory; the quantity in the supply pipeline between order and receipt. | Pipeline stock = average demand rate x lead time. |
| **Hedge stock** | — | Inventory purchased in advance of expected price increases, supply disruptions, or material shortages. | "We're building hedge stock before the tariffs hit." |
| **DOS** | Days of Supply | Current inventory divided by average daily demand; how many days the current stock will last. | "We have 45 DOS on that SKU." Also called "days on hand" or "weeks of supply." |
| **DOC** | Days of Coverage | Similar to DOS but can be calculated forward against the demand plan rather than historical averages. | "DOC against the forecast shows we'll cover 30 days." |
| **Inventory turns** | — | Annual cost of goods sold divided by average inventory value; measures how many times inventory cycles through per year. | "Our turns went from 6 to 8 — we're leaner." Higher is generally better. |
| **GMROI** | Gross Margin Return on Investment (Inventory) | Gross margin divided by average inventory cost; measures profitability per dollar of inventory investment. | "GMROI of 2.5 means we earn $2.50 in margin per dollar of inventory." |
| **Fill rate** | — | The percentage of customer order lines or units that can be filled immediately from on-hand inventory without backorder. | "Our fill rate dropped to 92% — we're shorting 8% of demand." |
| **Service level** | SL | The probability of not stocking out during a replenishment cycle (Cycle Service Level) or the proportion of demand met from stock (Fill Rate). | Ambiguous: always clarify whether CSL (probability) or fill rate (proportion). "Target 95% SL." |
| **CSL** | Cycle Service Level | The probability that inventory does not reach zero during a single replenishment cycle; also called Type 1 service level or alpha service level. | CSL = P(no stockout in a cycle). Set by z-score in safety stock formula. |
| **Stockout** | — | A condition where available inventory reaches zero and demand cannot be fulfilled; results in either backorder or lost sale. | "We stocked out on Tuesday." Conversational: "we're out." |
| **Backorder** | — | Unfulfilled demand that remains on the books to be shipped when inventory becomes available, rather than being cancelled. | "We have 200 units on backorder." Differs from lost sale. |
| **Lost sale** | — | Unfulfilled demand where the customer cancels or goes to a competitor rather than waiting for a backorder. | Not always visible in data — you see the stockout but not the lost demand. |
| **Overstock** | — | Inventory significantly above the maximum target level or above what can be consumed within a reasonable time. | "We're overstocked — we have 90 DOS against a target of 30." |
| **Obsolescence** | — | Inventory that can no longer be sold or used due to product changes, expiration, or market shifts; must be written off. | "We need to write off $200K in obsolete inventory." |
| **Shrinkage** | — | Inventory loss from theft, damage, miscounting, or process errors; the difference between system quantity and physical count. | "Shrinkage is running at 1.2% — we need a cycle count." |
| **Reorder point** | ROP | The inventory level at which a new replenishment order should be placed; typically = demand during lead time + safety stock. | "The ROP is 500 units." When on-hand drops to ROP, trigger an order. |
| **EOQ** | Economic Order Quantity | The order quantity that minimizes the sum of ordering costs and holding costs; classical lot-sizing formula = sqrt(2DS/H). | Often a starting point, rarely used exactly in practice. D=annual demand, S=order cost, H=holding cost/unit/year. |
| **MOQ** | Minimum Order Quantity | The smallest quantity a supplier will accept on a purchase order. | "The MOQ is 1,000 units — we can't order less." A hard constraint on ordering. |
| **Lot size** | — | The quantity ordered or produced in a single replenishment batch; determined by lot-sizing rules (EOQ, fixed, POQ, LFL). | "Lot size of 500" = we always order/produce in batches of 500. |
| **LFL** | Lot-for-Lot | Lot-sizing policy where the order quantity exactly matches the net requirement in each period; minimizes inventory but maximizes order frequency. | "We're running LFL on that component — no batching." |
| **POQ** | Period Order Quantity | Lot-sizing policy that orders enough to cover a fixed number of periods of demand; balances ordering and holding costs. | "POQ of 4 weeks" = each order covers 4 weeks of requirements. |
| **PPB** | Part Period Balancing | Lot-sizing heuristic that accumulates requirements until cumulative holding cost approaches the ordering cost. | Dynamic lot sizing — adapts to demand variability. |
| **FIFO** | First In, First Out | Inventory valuation and physical flow method where the oldest stock is consumed or shipped first. | Standard for most industries. "Pull from the oldest lot." |
| **LIFO** | Last In, First Out | Inventory valuation method where the newest stock is consumed first; rare in physical operations, used for tax purposes. | Largely a financial accounting method; not common in physical warehouse ops. |
| **FEFO** | First Expired, First Out | Inventory flow method where items closest to expiration are consumed first; required for food, pharma, and perishables. | "We run FEFO — expiry date drives the pick sequence." |
| **ABC analysis** | — | Classification of inventory items by value contribution (A=top 80% of value, B=next 15%, C=bottom 5%) to prioritize management attention. | "A-items get daily review, C-items get monthly." Lokad considers this an antipattern when used for policy setting. |
| **XYZ analysis** | — | Classification by demand variability (X=stable/predictable, Y=moderate variability, Z=highly erratic); often combined with ABC. | "AX items are high-value and predictable — easy to plan. CZ items are low-value and erratic." |
| **VMI** | Vendor Managed Inventory | Arrangement where the supplier monitors the customer's inventory and makes replenishment decisions, often shipping against consignment stock. | "We're on VMI with that supplier — they own the replenishment decision." |
| **Consignment** | — | Inventory physically located at the customer's site but still owned by the supplier until consumed; the supplier carries the inventory risk. | Often paired with VMI. "Consignment stock — we don't pay until we use it." |
| **MEIO** | Multi-Echelon Inventory Optimization | Optimizes safety stock across the entire network simultaneously rather than independently at each location; captures the fact that upstream buffers protect downstream locations. | Advanced inventory approach (tools: LLamasoft, Coupa, o9). "MEIO reduced our total SS by 20% vs. single-echelon." |
| **DDMRP** | Demand Driven MRP | Planning methodology that positions strategic decoupling buffers in the network and uses actual demand (not forecast) to drive replenishment through color-coded buffer zones (red/yellow/green). | Alternative to traditional MRP+safety stock. Demand Driven Institute certification. |

## 4. Order Types & Order Management

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **PO** | Purchase Order | An order placed with an external supplier to procure materials or goods; generated by MRP or manual procurement. | "Place the PO" = send the order to the supplier. "The PO is late" = supplier hasn't delivered on time. |
| **MO** | Manufacturing Order | An order to produce a specified quantity of a product at a manufacturing facility; also called production order or work order. | "Release the MO" = authorize production to start. "The MO is on the floor." |
| **TO** | Transfer Order | An order to move inventory from one internal location to another (plant to DC, DC to DC); generated by DRP or manual planning. | "Create a TO to move 500 units from CDC to the East RDC." |
| **SO** | Sales Order | A customer order for product delivery; the demand-side counterpart to PO/MO/TO on the supply side. | "We have a big SO coming in" = large customer order. |
| **Planned order** | — | An MRP/DRP-generated recommendation for a future PO, MO, or TO that has not yet been released (firmed or converted). | "Planned orders need to be firmed and released." MRP output before human approval. |
| **Firmed order** | — | A planned order that a planner has locked so MRP will not reschedule or delete it; ready for release. | "Firm the planned orders inside the planning time fence." |
| **Released order** | — | An order that has been authorized for execution — PO sent to supplier, MO released to shop floor, TO dispatched. | "Released" means execution is underway. |
| **Open order** | — | Any order that has been released but not yet fully received or completed. | "How many open POs do we have with that supplier?" |
| **Scheduled receipt** | — | An open order (PO, MO, or TO) that is expected to arrive in a specific future period; MRP uses these in the netting calculation. | "We have a scheduled receipt of 1,000 units in week 12." |
| **Backlog** | — | The accumulation of unfulfilled orders (customer or internal) that are past due or awaiting capacity/inventory to fulfill. | "The backlog is growing — we're 3 weeks behind." |
| **Order promising** | — | The process of committing a delivery date to a customer based on available inventory and planned supply. | Uses ATP, CTP, or AATP logic. "When can we promise this order?" |
| **ASN** | Advanced Shipping Notice | Electronic notification from a supplier that a shipment has been dispatched, containing contents, quantities, and expected arrival. | "The ASN says it ships tomorrow, arriving Friday." |
| **GR** | Goods Receipt | The physical and system receipt of materials into inventory at a receiving location; triggers inventory update and invoice matching. | "Post the GR" = record the receipt in the system. |
| **3-way match** | — | Verification that the PO, goods receipt, and supplier invoice all agree on quantity and price before payment is released. | "The 3-way match failed on the price — invoice is $2 higher than PO." |

## 5. Order Promising & Availability

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **ATP** | Available-to-Promise | The uncommitted portion of current inventory and planned production that can be promised to new customer orders. | "What's the ATP for next week?" = how much can we still promise. ATP = supply - committed demand. |
| **CTP** | Capable-to-Promise | Extends ATP by checking whether additional production, procurement, or transfers can fulfill demand that exceeds current ATP. | "ATP is zero but CTP shows we can make it if we expedite the raw material." |
| **AATP** | Allocated ATP | ATP with priority-based allocation by customer segment, channel, or product grouping; ensures high-priority customers get access first. | "AATP reserves 60% for Tier 1 customers before lower priorities can consume." |
| **PTF** | Planning Time Fence | The horizon beyond which MRP can automatically reschedule orders; inside the PTF, changes require planner approval. | "Don't change the MPS inside the PTF without approval." |
| **DTF** | Demand Time Fence | The near-term horizon within which only actual customer orders (not forecasts) drive the MPS, preventing forecast nervousness in execution. | "Inside the DTF, we're order-driven only." |

## 6. Supply Planning & Procurement

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Supply plan** | — | The plan specifying what to procure, produce, and transfer (quantities, sources, timing) to meet the demand plan within constraints. | "The supply plan says we need to start building in week 8." |
| **Net requirements** | — | Gross requirements minus on-hand inventory minus scheduled receipts; the quantity that must be supplied. | "After netting, we still need 300 units in week 14." |
| **BOM** | Bill of Materials | A structured list of all components, sub-assemblies, and raw materials needed to produce one unit of a parent product, with quantities per. | "Check the BOM — it requires 4 components per finished unit." Multi-level BOMs have parent-child hierarchies. |
| **BOM explosion** | — | The MRP process of multiplying parent-level requirements by BOM quantities to calculate dependent demand at each component level. | "BOM explosion drives the component requirements." |
| **Sourcing rule** | — | A rule specifying which supplier, plant, or transfer source should fulfill demand for a product at a location, with priorities and split percentages. | "The sourcing rule says 70% from Supplier A, 30% from Supplier B." Three types: buy, make, transfer. |
| **Lead time** | LT | The total elapsed time from placing an order to receiving the goods; includes supplier processing, manufacturing, transit, and receiving. | AMBIGUOUS — always ask which type. See "Ambiguous Terms" section below. |
| **Procurement lead time** | — | Time from PO placement to goods receipt at the buyer's dock; includes supplier production, shipping, and receiving. | "Procurement lead time for that component is 6 weeks." |
| **Manufacturing lead time** | MLT | Time from MO release to finished goods completion; includes queue, setup, run, wait, and move times. | "Manufacturing lead time is 3 days per batch." |
| **Cumulative lead time** | — | The longest path through the BOM from raw material procurement to finished goods; determines the earliest possible delivery for a new order. | "Our cumulative lead time is 12 weeks — anything inside that horizon requires inventory." |
| **Expedite** | — | To accelerate an order by paying premium freight, authorizing overtime, or reprioritizing production to meet an earlier need date. | "We need to expedite that PO — can the supplier ship air freight?" |
| **De-expedite** | — | To push out or slow down an order that is no longer needed on the original date, freeing capacity or avoiding excess inventory. | "De-expedite the MO — the customer pushed out delivery by 2 weeks." |
| **Make-vs-buy** | — | The decision whether to produce a component internally or purchase it from an external supplier, based on cost, capacity, capability, and strategic factors. | "The make-vs-buy analysis says outsourcing saves 15% but adds 3 weeks lead time." |
| **Sole source** | — | A product or material that can only be procured from a single supplier, creating supply risk. | "That's a sole-sourced component — if the supplier goes down, we're stuck." |
| **Dual source** | — | Deliberately qualifying two suppliers for the same material to reduce concentration risk. | "We need to dual-source that critical component." |
| **Blanket PO** | — | A long-term purchase agreement with a supplier covering an extended period, with releases against it as needed. | "We have a blanket PO — just issue a release for 500 units." |
| **Spot buy** | — | A one-time purchase outside of normal contracts, typically at a premium price, to cover an urgent shortfall. | "We had to spot-buy from a broker at 40% above contract price." |
| **TCO** | Total Cost of Ownership | The full cost of procurement including unit price, freight, duties, quality costs, inventory carrying costs, and administration. | "The cheapest unit price isn't always the lowest TCO." |
| **COGS** | Cost of Goods Sold | The direct costs attributable to producing the goods sold, including materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead. | Financial metric, not a planning term per se, but planners reference it constantly. |

## 7. Manufacturing & Production

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **WIP** | Work in Process | Inventory that is currently being manufactured or assembled but is not yet a finished product. | "We have $2M in WIP on the shop floor." |
| **FG** | Finished Goods | Completed products ready for sale or shipment to customers. | "FG inventory at the DC." |
| **RM** | Raw Materials | Purchased materials and components that have not yet entered the manufacturing process. | "RM stock levels are low — we need the PO to arrive." |
| **SFG** | Semi-Finished Goods | Intermediate products that have completed some but not all manufacturing steps; also called sub-assemblies or intermediates. | "We hold SFG at the postponement point." |
| **MTS** | Make-to-Stock | Production strategy where products are manufactured to forecast and held in inventory for immediate customer delivery. | "This is an MTS item — we build to forecast." Short lead times to customer, higher inventory risk. |
| **MTO** | Make-to-Order | Production strategy where manufacturing starts only after a customer order is received. | "This is MTO — we don't build until we have an SO." Longer lead times, lower inventory risk. |
| **ATO** | Assemble-to-Order | Hybrid strategy where standard components are pre-built to stock, then final assembly/configuration occurs after the customer order. | "We hold the base unit as SFG and ATO with the customer's configuration." Postponement strategy. |
| **ETO** | Engineer-to-Order | Production strategy where product design and engineering happen after the customer order; highly customized, longest lead time. | "ETO projects — each one is unique." |
| **Postponement** | — | Strategy of delaying product differentiation (final assembly, labeling, configuration) as late as possible to reduce forecast risk. | "We use postponement — build generic modules, customize at the DC." |
| **OEE** | Overall Equipment Effectiveness | Composite metric = Availability x Performance x Quality; measures how effectively a manufacturing operation is utilized. | "OEE of 72% means we're losing 28% to downtime, speed loss, and defects." World-class is 85%+. |
| **Takt time** | — | The rate at which finished products must be completed to meet customer demand; available production time / customer demand rate. | "Takt time is 2 minutes — we need to complete one unit every 2 minutes." |
| **Cycle time** | — | The actual time to complete one unit or one operation; should be less than or equal to takt time. | AMBIGUOUS — in manufacturing, it means production time per unit. In supply chain, it may mean the total order cycle. |
| **Changeover** | — | The time and process required to switch a production line or machine from one product to another; also called setup time. | "Changeover takes 4 hours — we need to minimize the number of changeovers." |
| **SMED** | Single-Minute Exchange of Die | Lean methodology for reducing changeover/setup time to under 10 minutes ("single-minute" = single digit). | "We did a SMED event and cut changeover from 90 minutes to 8." |
| **Bottleneck** | — | The resource or process step with the lowest capacity that constrains the throughput of the entire system. | "The paint line is the bottleneck — everything queues there." Theory of Constraints focuses on the bottleneck. |
| **Throughput** | — | The rate at which the system produces output (finished goods or orders) per unit of time. | "Throughput is 500 units per shift." |
| **Yield** | — | The proportion of good output from a manufacturing process, accounting for scrap, rework, and defects. | "Yield is 95% — plan for 5% scrap." MRP should gross up requirements by 1/yield. |
| **Scrap** | — | Material or product that is defective and cannot be reworked; must be disposed of. | "We scrapped 200 units in that batch." |
| **Rework** | — | Processing a defective item again to bring it to acceptable quality; consumes additional capacity and materials. | "30 units need rework — that's 2 more hours on the line." |
| **Kanban** | — | A pull-based replenishment system using visual signals (cards, bins, electronic signals) to authorize production or movement of material only when consumed downstream. | "We run Kanban on the shop floor — when a bin empties, it triggers a replenishment." |
| **JIT** | Just-in-Time | Manufacturing philosophy that produces only what is needed, when it is needed, in the quantity needed; minimizes inventory and waste. | "JIT delivery means the components arrive the same day they're needed on the line." |
| **JIS** | Just-in-Sequence | Extension of JIT where parts are delivered not only at the right time but in the exact production sequence required on the assembly line. | Automotive industry: "JIS delivery — seats arrive in color sequence matching the build order." |
| **TPM** | Total Productive Maintenance | Maintenance strategy involving all employees in proactive and preventive maintenance to maximize equipment effectiveness. | "TPM reduced our unplanned downtime by 40%." |
| **Glenday Sieve** | — | Method of classifying products by production volume to optimize scheduling: top ~6% of SKUs (green) get dedicated fixed repeating schedule, reducing changeovers. | "Green products run every cycle; amber products run when capacity allows." |
| **BOS** | Bill of Service | Similar to BOM but defines the resources, skills, and time required to deliver a service rather than a physical product. | Less common; used in service-oriented supply chains. |

## 8. Logistics & Distribution

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **DC** | Distribution Center | A warehouse facility focused on receiving, storing, and shipping goods; typically does not manufacture. | General term for any distribution warehouse. |
| **CDC** | Central Distribution Center | The primary hub in a distribution network, receiving directly from manufacturing and feeding regional DCs. | Also called NDC (National DC). "Everything flows through the CDC first." |
| **RDC** | Regional Distribution Center | A distribution facility serving a geographic region, receiving from the CDC and shipping to local facilities or customers. | "The East Coast RDC covers New England and Mid-Atlantic." |
| **NDC** | National Distribution Center | Synonym for CDC; the main hub for a country-wide distribution network. | Common in European supply chains. |
| **3PL** | Third-Party Logistics | An external company that provides outsourced logistics services including warehousing, transportation, and fulfillment. | "We use a 3PL for our West Coast distribution." |
| **4PL** | Fourth-Party Logistics | A logistics integrator that manages multiple 3PLs and the entire logistics network on behalf of the client. | "The 4PL coordinates across our 3 warehousing partners." |
| **LTL** | Less-Than-Truckload | Shipping mode where freight from multiple shippers is consolidated into a single truck; for shipments too large for parcel but too small for a full truck. | "That order is going LTL — about 6 pallets." |
| **FTL** | Full Truckload | Shipping mode where a single shipper fills an entire truck; lower per-unit cost than LTL for large shipments. | "We're shipping FTL — 24 pallets." |
| **Cross-docking** | — | Distribution method where incoming goods are transferred directly from receiving to shipping with minimal storage, bypassing warehousing. | "High-velocity items get cross-docked — they never hit a rack." |
| **Last mile** | — | The final leg of delivery from the last distribution point to the end customer; typically the most expensive per-unit segment. | "Last mile delivery costs are killing our margins." |
| **Milk run** | — | A scheduled route that picks up or delivers to multiple locations in sequence, like a milk delivery route. | "The milk run hits 5 suppliers and brings components back to the plant daily." |
| **Dead stock** | — | Inventory that has had no demand or movement for an extended period (typically 6-12 months); candidate for liquidation or write-off. | "10% of our warehouse is dead stock." |
| **Cube utilization** | — | The percentage of available warehouse volume (cubic space) that is actually occupied by stored inventory. | "Cube utilization is at 92% — we need more space or to reduce stock." |
| **Pick/Pack/Ship** | — | The warehouse fulfillment process: pick items from storage locations, pack them for shipment, and ship to the customer. | "Pick/pack/ship takes 24 hours for standard orders." |
| **Wave planning** | — | Grouping warehouse pick/pack orders into batches (waves) to optimize labor and equipment utilization. | "We run 3 waves per day — morning, afternoon, and evening cut." |
| **Dwell time** | — | The time inventory sits idle at a location before being moved, consumed, or shipped; excessive dwell increases costs and risk. | "Dwell time at the port is 5 days — we need to clear customs faster." |

## 9. SCOR Metrics & Performance

| Term | Full Name | Definition | Context |
|------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **OTIF** | On-Time In-Full | The percentage of customer orders delivered both on or before the requested date AND in the full quantity ordered; the primary customer service metric. | "Our OTIF is 93% — target is 97%." Also called DIFOT (Delivered In Full On Time). |
| **POF** | Perfect Order Fulfillment | SCOR Level 1 metric: the percentage of orders that are on-time, in-full, damage-free, and with correct documentation — all four conditions must be met. | "POF is always lower than OTIF because it's more stringent." |
| **OFCT** | Order Fulfillment Cycle Time | Average elapsed time from customer order receipt to delivery at the customer's dock. | "Our OFCT is 5 days — industry benchmark is 3." |
| **C2C** | Cash-to-Cash Cycle Time | DIO + DSO - DPO; the number of days between paying suppliers and collecting from customers. | "Our C2C is 45 days — we want to get it under 30." Lower is better (less working capital tied up). |
| **DIO** | Days Inventory Outstanding | Average inventory / (COGS / 365); how many days of cost of goods sold are sitting in inventory. | "DIO of 60 means we're carrying 2 months of inventory." |
| **DSO** | Days Sales Outstanding | Accounts receivable / (Revenue / 365); how many days it takes to collect payment from customers. | "DSO of 35 means customers pay in about 5 weeks." |
| **DPO** | Days Payable Outstanding | Accounts payable / (COGS / 365); how many days we take to pay our suppliers. | "DPO of 60 means we pay suppliers in 2 months." Higher DPO preserves cash but strains supplier relationships. |
| **OTD** | On-Time Delivery | The percentage of deliveries (inbound or outbound) that arrive on or before the committed date. | "Supplier OTD is 94%." Can refer to either supplier performance or our delivery performance. |
| **TCTS** | Total Cost to Serve | The total supply chain cost as a percentage of revenue, including procurement, manufacturing, logistics, warehousing, and administration. | SCOR Level 1 cost metric. "Our TCTS is 8% of revenue." |
| **Bullwhip effect** | — | The amplification of demand variability as you move upstream in the supply chain; small fluctuations at retail become large swings at the factory. | "The distributor is over-ordering — classic bullwhip." Caused by batching, lead times, rationing, and price fluctuations. |
| **Bullwhip ratio** | — | The ratio of order variability to demand variability (variance of orders / variance of demand); a value > 1.0 indicates bullwhip amplification. | "A bullwhip ratio of 2.5 means order variability is 2.5x the demand variability." |
