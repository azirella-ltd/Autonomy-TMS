# Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP) and Integrated Business Planning (IBP)

## Comprehensive Reference Guide for Supply Chain Planning

---

## 1. Definition and Purpose

**Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP)** is a cross-functional business management process that aligns demand, supply, and financial plans within the context of the company's strategic plan. S&OP operates on a monthly cycle and produces a single, agreed-upon operating plan that balances demand and supply across a rolling 18-36 month planning horizon.

**Integrated Business Planning (IBP)** is the evolution of S&OP. Developed by Oliver Wight, IBP extends S&OP by integrating financial planning, product portfolio management, and strategic planning into a single decision-making process. IBP connects strategy to execution by ensuring all functional plans (demand, supply, inventory, new products, financial) are aligned and reconciled monthly.

---

## 2. S&OP Process Steps (Oliver Wight Methodology)

### Step 1: Product Management Review
- Review product portfolio: new product introductions, phase-outs, cannibalization
- Assess product lifecycle stages and transition plans
- Update product roadmap and ensure alignment with strategic plan
- Key output: Updated product portfolio assumptions for next 24+ months

### Step 2: Demand Review
- Develop unconstrained demand forecast using statistical methods + market intelligence
- Incorporate sales pipeline, customer forecasts, marketing plans, promotions
- Identify demand risks and opportunities (upside/downside scenarios)
- Key output: Consensus demand plan (units and revenue) with assumptions documented
- Metrics: Forecast accuracy (MAPE, WMAPE), forecast bias, forecast value added (FVA)

### Step 3: Supply Review
- Evaluate capacity to meet the demand plan across all resources
- Identify supply constraints: production capacity, material availability, supplier capacity, logistics
- Develop supply alternatives: overtime, outsourcing, inventory build, capital investment
- Key output: Constrained supply plan with gap analysis and proposed solutions

### Step 4: Integrated Reconciliation (Pre-S&OP)
- Cross-functional review of demand-supply gaps
- Evaluate financial impact of alternatives (revenue, cost, margin, working capital)
- Prepare decision recommendations with scenarios for executive review
- Key output: Recommended operating plan with financial implications

### Step 5: Executive S&OP / Management Business Review
- Senior leadership reviews and approves the integrated plan
- Makes decisions on demand-supply gaps, resource investments, strategic trade-offs
- Approves the single operating plan that drives execution
- Key output: Authorized operating plan, updated financial forecast, action items

## 3. S&OP Maturity Levels

### Oliver Wight Class A-D Assessment

| Class | Level | Characteristics |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **Class D** | Reactive | No formal process, firefighting, spreadsheet-based, functional silos |
| **Class C** | Standard | Basic S&OP in place, monthly meetings, limited cross-functional participation |
| **Class B** | Advanced | Effective S&OP process, good data, scenario planning, financial integration |
| **Class A** | Excellence | Full IBP, strategy-connected, proactive decision-making, continuous improvement |

### Gartner 5-Stage Maturity Model

| Stage | Name | Key Characteristics |
|-------|------|---------------------|
| 1 | **React** | Spreadsheet-based, siloed functions, no formal S&OP, firefighting mode |
| 2 | **Anticipate** | Basic demand planning, simple S&OP process, functional optimization, batch cycles |
| 3 | **Integrate** | Cross-functional S&OP/IBP, demand-supply balancing, scenario planning |
| 4 | **Collaborate** | Multi-enterprise collaboration, demand sensing, probabilistic planning |
| 5 | **Orchestrate** | Autonomous planning, self-healing supply chains, AI-driven, digital twins |

## 4. From S&OP to IBP: Key Differences

| Dimension | Traditional S&OP | Integrated Business Planning |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| **Scope** | Demand-supply balance | Strategy to execution alignment |
| **Horizon** | 12-18 months rolling | 24-36+ months rolling |
| **Financial integration** | Limited (unit plans only) | Full P&L, balance sheet, cash flow |
| **Product portfolio** | Separate process | Integrated product review |
| **Strategic alignment** | Loose connection | Direct link to strategic plan |
| **Granularity** | Aggregate families | Multiple levels (strategic → operational) |
| **Scenario planning** | Ad hoc | Structured what-if analysis |
| **Participants** | Supply chain + sales | All functions including finance, R&D |
| **Decision quality** | Operational | Strategic and operational |
| **Technology** | Spreadsheets / basic APS | Advanced planning platforms (IBP tools) |

## 5. IBP Process Components

### 5.1 Product Portfolio Review
- New product introductions (NPI): launch dates, ramp-up curves, cannibalization
- Product phase-outs: end-of-life planning, inventory run-down
- Portfolio rationalization: SKU complexity management
- Technology transitions: platform changes, component substitutions

### 5.2 Demand Review
- Statistical baseline forecast (time series: exponential smoothing, ARIMA)
- Demand sensing (short-term signal processing: POS, weather, social)
- Consensus building across sales, marketing, customer insights
- Demand shaping: pricing, promotions, channel incentives
- Scenario analysis: base case, upside, downside with probability weighting

### 5.3 Supply Review
- Rough-cut capacity planning (RCCP) against demand plan
- Resource availability: labor, equipment, materials, logistics
- Make-vs-buy decisions and outsourcing options
- Supplier capacity assessment and constraint identification
- Capital expenditure implications

### 5.4 Financial Review
- Revenue forecast reconciliation with demand plan
- Cost impact of supply alternatives (variable, fixed, capital)
- Working capital impact (inventory investment, payables, receivables)
- Margin analysis by product family, channel, geography
- Gap to budget/target identification

### 5.5 Integrated Reconciliation
- Demand-supply gap closure recommendations
- Financial trade-off analysis (service vs cost vs cash — Oliver Wyman Triangle)
- Risk assessment and mitigation plans
- Action items and accountability assignment

### 5.6 Management Business Review
- Executive decision-making on unresolved gaps
- Strategic direction confirmation or adjustment
- Resource allocation and investment decisions
- Communication of approved plan to organization

## 6. Key Metrics for S&OP/IBP

### Demand Side
- **Forecast Accuracy**: 1 - |Actual - Forecast| / Actual (measured at family level)
- **WMAPE**: Weighted Mean Absolute Percentage Error
- **Forecast Bias**: Systematic over/under forecasting trend
- **FVA (Forecast Value Added)**: Improvement from each step in forecast process

### Supply Side
- **Capacity Utilization**: Actual output / Maximum capacity
- **Schedule Adherence**: Actual production vs planned production
- **Supplier On-Time Delivery**: % of POs delivered on or before due date

### Financial
- **Revenue Plan Accuracy**: Actual revenue vs S&OP revenue plan
- **Inventory Investment**: Actual vs planned inventory value
- **Working Capital**: Days of inventory + Days receivable - Days payable

### Process
- **Plan Stability**: % change in plan month-over-month (lower is better)
- **Assumption Accuracy**: % of key assumptions that materialized
- **Decision Effectiveness**: % of S&OP decisions implemented on time

## 7. Common S&OP Failures and How to Avoid Them

1. **Lack of executive sponsorship**: S&OP must be owned by the CEO or COO, not delegated to supply chain alone
2. **Data-poor discussions**: Meetings without data become opinion contests; invest in planning tools
3. **Too much detail**: S&OP operates at aggregate level; don't attempt SKU-level discussion
4. **No financial integration**: Plans in units only fail to drive business decisions
5. **Skipping steps**: Each process step builds on the previous; shortcutting undermines quality
6. **No accountability**: Action items without owners and deadlines die
7. **Stale assumptions**: Review and update planning assumptions monthly
8. **Firefighting in S&OP meetings**: Operational issues should be resolved outside S&OP

## 8. Planning Horizons and Time Fences

| Zone | Horizon | Decision Type | Flexibility |
|------|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Frozen** | 0-4 weeks | Execution only | Very low — changes require escalation |
| **Firm** | 4-8 weeks | Minor adjustments | Low — limited scope changes |
| **Planning** | 2-6 months | Full replanning | Medium — volume and mix changes |
| **Strategic** | 6-36 months | Capacity/investment | High — structural changes |

## 10. Relationship to Other Planning Processes

```
Strategic Plan (3-5 years)
    ↓
S&OP / IBP (18-36 months, monthly cycle)
    ↓
Master Production Schedule (MPS) (3-18 months, weekly)
    ↓
Material Requirements Planning (MRP) (1-6 months, daily/weekly)
    ↓
Execution (Purchase Orders, Production Orders, Transfer Orders)
```

S&OP/IBP provides the **demand signal** and **capacity authorization** that drives MPS. MPS decomposes the aggregate S&OP plan into product-level production schedules. MRP further explodes MPS into component-level material requirements.
